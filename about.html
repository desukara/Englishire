About Englishire

Continuity deserves more than a hurried answer.

Englishire was established to help schools preserve the quiet order of education when circumstances require a temporary change of teacher.

Our work is shaped by Tokyo classrooms, Japanese learners and a simple conviction: temporary cover should support what a school has carefully built, not oblige it to lower its expectations merely because the timetable has become difficult.

Why We Exist

Schools deserve breathing room.

Every school develops its own character over time. Its routines become familiar. Its expectations become understood. Pupils recognise the people who greet them, the classrooms in which they learn and the standards which quietly shape each school day.

A teacher's absence inevitably interrupts that continuity. It need not diminish it.

Englishire was established from a practical observation. Schools rarely lack goodwill when a staffing difficulty arises. What they lack is time. An absence may require an answer before the first lesson. A recruitment process may take longer than expected. A growing timetable may ask more of the existing team than can reasonably be sustained.

Yet the sound permanent decision is seldom the one made in haste. Proper temporary cover gives a school room to think, recruit and plan without allowing the quality of the school day to become the price of that time.

Temporary cover should relieve pressure without lowering standards.

The Observation Behind Englishire

The difficulty was never simply finding a teacher.

The real difficulty was finding the right teacher, with sufficient understanding, in the time available.

A free diary does not by itself make a person suitable for a classroom. The school, the learners, the timetable, the lesson format, the journey and the responsibilities attached to the assignment all matter.

Englishire grew from the belief that these details should be considered before an undertaking is accepted, not discovered after a teacher has arrived. That belief remains the basis of the organisation.

The school is not interchangeable
Each organisation has its own culture, routines and reasonable expectations of professional conduct.
The learner is not a timetable entry
Age, confidence, level and prior classroom experience shape how a lesson ought to be approached.
The assignment is not merely a vacancy
A single day, recurring timetable and extended placement demand different forms of preparation and judgement.
Urgency does not remove responsibility
Indeed, the less time available, the more valuable calm and accurate judgement become.

Experience Without Fanfare

Experience is best recognised in the details.

It reveals itself less in declarations than in the questions asked before a promise is made.

Englishire's approach has been shaped across the varied landscape of English education in Tokyo: independent language schools, children's programmes, private and international settings, adult classes, specialist courses and other organisations with their own methods and expectations.

That breadth matters because no universal formula can serve every classroom. A lively afternoon class for young children bears little resemblance to an evening programme for working adults. A formal academic setting asks something different of a teacher than a conversational school with a highly personal atmosphere.

Familiarity with these distinctions does not encourage assumption. Quite the reverse. It teaches one to ask better questions, notice practical risks and recognise when an arrangement which appears straightforward requires rather more thought.

Understanding Japanese Learners

Knowing English is not the same as knowing how to teach it here.

A capable teacher reads the room as carefully as the lesson plan.

01

Confidence before display

Some learners speak readily. Others require time and reassurance before they are willing to take a linguistic risk in public.

Participation should not be measured solely by who speaks first or loudest.

02

Structure without rigidity

Clear expectations and an orderly lesson often create the security from which genuine participation becomes possible.

Warmth and classroom command are not opposites. Good teaching frequently requires both.

03

Respect for purpose

Children, teenagers, adults and specialist learners do not arrive with identical aims or expectations.

The reason for studying English should influence the manner in which it is taught.

04

Continuity with what came before

A temporary teacher should understand where the class has been, what it is working towards and how the school ordinarily supports progress.

The object is not to perform a disconnected lesson, but to serve the course already in progress.

Our Written Standard

The principles are not separate from the work.

The Englishire Standard is the written expression of how we believe professional teacher cover ought to be approached. It describes the responsibilities which precede an assignment, the conduct expected during it and the judgement required when circumstances change.

It is neither a slogan nor a collection of extravagant promises. It exists so that schools, teachers and Englishire itself may understand the principles against which each decision should be considered.

Our Responsibility

Trust should be answered with proportionate care.

Schools entrust temporary teachers with far more than a vacant hour in the timetable.

To the school

We owe clear communication, realistic expectations and respect for the organisation's established procedures and culture.

To the learner

We owe purposeful teaching, calm conduct and recognition that a change of teacher should not make the school day feel careless.

To the teacher

We owe sufficient information, a proper briefing and an honest account of the assignment before responsibility is accepted.

What We Believe

Reasonable principles, consistently applied.

Not grand declarations. Simply the habits required to conduct temporary teacher cover properly.

Clarity should precede confirmation

Dates, times, location, learners, lesson format, materials and expectations should be understood before an assignment is agreed.

Urgency should not disguise uncertainty

Where information is incomplete, the uncertainty should be acknowledged rather than concealed beneath a convenient assurance.

A temporary teacher should respect what already exists

The task is to support the school's routines, relationships and educational aims, not to arrive determined to replace them with a personal system.

Professionalism is generally quiet

It is found in punctuality, preparation, sensible communication, confidentiality and composed classroom conduct rather than theatrical promises.

There are occasions when the proper answer is no

An assignment should be declined where it cannot be undertaken suitably, punctually or with the level of preparation the circumstances require.
