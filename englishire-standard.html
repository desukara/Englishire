A Published Statement of Practice

The Englishire Standard

Principles for professional teacher cover in Japanese schools.

A school does not set aside its standards merely because a member of staff is absent. The Englishire Standard describes the care, judgement and professional conduct that ought to accompany every temporary teaching assignment.

Preamble

An absence may alter the timetable. It need not diminish the school day.

A teacher’s absence should never diminish a school’s commitment to learning.

Whether anticipated or unexpected, every absence presents a challenge which extends beyond the timetable. Lessons continue. Pupils continue. Expectations remain. Parents retain confidence that their children will experience a purposeful school day, and colleagues continue to rely upon one another to maintain the standards which define their community.

Professional teacher cover exists to protect that continuity. Its purpose is not merely to place an adult in front of a classroom, nor simply to complete a vacant line on a schedule. It is to preserve, as far as circumstances allow, the quality, stability and confidence upon which effective schools depend.

This Standard sets out the principles which guide Englishire’s approach to temporary teacher cover in Japan. It is neither a catalogue of claims nor a promise that every circumstance can be made uncomplicated. It is a practical account of the judgement, preparation and conduct which should accompany responsible work in a school.

Each school possesses its own character, routines and educational priorities. Those distinctions matter. Yet certain principles remain constant: careful preparation is preferable to hurried decisions; honest communication is preferable to unnecessary certainty; suitability should take precedence over convenience; and professional judgement should not be abandoned simply because time is short.

Effective cover begins before a teacher enters the classroom. It begins with understanding the school, the pupils, the timetable, the expectations of leadership and the practical realities of the assignment. Every subsequent decision ought to strengthen that understanding rather than diminish it.

The objective is straightforward: to help schools preserve continuity, uphold educational standards and maintain confidence when circumstances require flexibility.

Principle I

The Purpose of Teacher Cover

Teacher cover exists to protect the continuity of education, not merely to occupy a classroom.

The presence of an adult is not, by itself, a continuation of education. A lesson has purpose, sequence and an established place within a wider programme of study. Pupils arrive with expectations formed by the work which preceded it, and their next lesson will depend, at least in part, upon what takes place in the present one.

Temporary cover should therefore be understood as an educational responsibility rather than an administrative convenience. Supervision may be one element of that responsibility, particularly where circumstances allow little notice, but it should not become its entire definition. The aim remains to preserve meaningful learning wherever the available information, time and resources make that reasonably possible.

This requires attention to more than lesson content. Classroom routines, behaviour expectations, safeguarding arrangements, pastoral considerations and the confidence of pupils all form part of continuity. A capable temporary teacher recognises that the school day is an organised whole. One lesson cannot be treated as though it exists in isolation from the institution around it.

Continuity is proportionate, not theatrical

Professional cover does not attempt to imitate the absent teacher, nor to introduce unnecessary novelty in order to make an impression. It seeks instead to preserve what matters: calm, purposeful work; appropriate expectations; and a clear handover which allows the permanent teacher or school team to continue without avoidable confusion.

The measure of successful cover is seldom spectacle. More often, it is that pupils knew what was expected, learning proceeded in an orderly fashion, and the school remained confident in what had taken place.

Principle II

Every Assignment Deserves Proper Consideration

An assignment should not be accepted before its essential requirements are understood.

Urgency is a feature of many staffing enquiries. It is not a reason to dispense with judgement. Before an assignment is confirmed, there should be a proper understanding of the dates, location, timetable, pupil age groups, lesson expectations and any duties which extend beyond classroom teaching.

The practical details matter because they shape the assignment. A day of primary provision differs materially from a secondary examination class. A familiar series of English lessons differs from a timetable divided across departments, campuses or age groups. Travel arrangements, arrival procedures and the time available between lessons may appear incidental, yet each can affect whether the work can be carried out properly.

Consideration also protects the school from assurances which ought never to have been given. A hurried acceptance followed by revision, substitution or withdrawal creates more difficulty than a measured answer provided at the outset. It is therefore entirely proper to ask questions, clarify assumptions and, where necessary, decline an assignment which cannot be undertaken to a suitable standard.

What should be understood before confirmation

  • The school location, reporting time and expected finish.
  • The dates, frequency and likely duration of the requirement.
  • The year groups, class sizes and general learner profile.
  • The subject content, materials and level of preparation available.
  • Any form duties, break duties, clubs, meetings or pastoral responsibilities.
  • The school’s safeguarding, behaviour and communication procedures.

Not every detail will be available at first contact, particularly during an unexpected absence. The standard is not that schools must produce perfect information. It is that both parties should distinguish clearly between what is known, what remains to be confirmed and what cannot reasonably be promised.

Principle III

Suitability Before Availability

The fact that a teacher is available does not, by itself, make that teacher suitable.

Availability answers only one question. Schools require rather more. The teacher must be able to work with the relevant age group, understand the nature of the curriculum, conduct the classroom with confidence and adapt appropriately to the school’s established expectations.

Suitability is not determined by a single credential or number of years in education. Experience is valuable, but its relevance must be considered. The calm judgement required in a junior classroom may differ from the subject confidence required in an upper-secondary lesson. Work with young learners, international programmes, examination preparation and adult education may all involve English teaching, yet they are not interchangeable.

Professional selection therefore concerns fit. It asks whether the teacher’s experience, disposition and practical abilities are appropriate for the particular assignment. It also asks whether the teacher can represent the school with discretion, follow instructions without unnecessary reinterpretation and recognise when a matter should be referred rather than resolved independently.

Suitability includes temperament

Schools are communities as well as workplaces. A temporary teacher enters established relationships and routines. The ability to listen carefully, remain composed, accept guidance and exercise proportionate authority is therefore as important as confidence at the front of a room.

A suitable teacher need not arrive claiming to possess every answer. It is generally more reassuring to encounter someone who understands the limits of the brief, asks sensible questions and carries out the work entrusted to them with care.

Principle IV

Preparation Protects the Classroom

Professional teaching begins before the first pupil enters the room.

Preparation is not an embellishment to temporary cover. It is one of the principal means by which uncertainty is reduced. A teacher who understands where to report, what is to be taught, which materials are available and whom to contact when a difficulty arises is better placed to begin the day calmly and to preserve that calm for pupils.

The briefing should be proportionate to the assignment. A single day may require concise but accurate information. A recurring or extended placement requires a fuller understanding of curriculum sequence, assessment, school systems, departmental expectations and the responsibilities which accompany membership of the wider staff team.

Preparation is shared work. The school provides the information and access reasonably available to it; the teacher reads what has been provided, seeks clarification where necessary and arrives ready to act upon it. Neither party benefits when important assumptions remain unspoken.

A sound briefing ordinarily addresses

  • Arrival, reception, identification and the principal school contact.
  • The timetable, rooms, transitions and arrangements for breaks.
  • Lesson plans, resources, technology and expected outcomes.
  • Behaviour routines, rewards, sanctions and referral procedures.
  • Safeguarding information and any relevant pupil considerations.
  • What should be recorded or handed over at the close of the day.

Where full lesson plans are unavailable, the absence of detail should be stated plainly. A sensible alternative can then be agreed. Improvisation may occasionally be necessary, but it should arise from informed judgement rather than preventable neglect.

Principle V

Professional Conduct Without Fanfare

Professionalism is most convincing when it is evident in ordinary conduct.

Schools should not have to request the elementary courtesies of professional life. Punctuality, suitable presentation, attentive communication and respect for confidentiality are not exceptional achievements. They are the proper foundation of work undertaken in the presence of children and on behalf of an educational institution.

A temporary teacher occupies a position of trust from the moment an assignment is accepted. That trust extends to the way school information is handled, how colleagues are addressed, how pupils are spoken about and the discretion shown outside the classroom. Familiarity with a school may grow over time; professional boundaries should remain clear throughout.

Conduct also includes reliability in small matters. Messages should be acknowledged. Changes should be communicated promptly. Difficulties should not be concealed in the hope that they will pass unnoticed. A school should receive an accurate account of what occurred, expressed with judgement and without unnecessary drama.

Authority should remain proportionate

A temporary teacher is expected to lead the classroom, but not to recast the institution. Existing routines and policies should be followed unless safety or professional duty requires otherwise. When uncertain, the proper course is usually to seek guidance from the person appointed by the school.

Quiet competence is preferable to conspicuous confidence. The purpose of the assignment is to support the school, not to make the temporary teacher its centre of attention.

Principle VI

Continuity of Learning

The lesson should leave the next lesson in a better position, not a more difficult one.

Temporary teaching takes place within an existing sequence. Even where the assignment is brief, the work completed, questions raised and difficulties encountered will affect what follows. A useful handover is therefore part of the teaching itself.

Pupils should know what has been accomplished and, where appropriate, what remains to be completed. The school or absent teacher should be able to see which material was covered, how the class responded and whether any matter requires attention. Notes need not be elaborate. They should be accurate, relevant and intelligible to the person who receives them.

Continuity also concerns expectations. Temporary cover should not become an invitation for standards of work or behaviour to fall away. Pupils are generally reassured by clarity. When routines are followed and the lesson is conducted with purpose, the temporary nature of the arrangement need not dominate the day.

Extended cover requires deeper continuity

As an assignment lengthens, responsibility grows. Planning, assessment, feedback, communication with colleagues and participation in the life of the school may become necessary. Those duties should be discussed explicitly rather than allowed to accumulate through assumption.

The aim is not to pretend that temporary and permanent roles are identical. It is to ensure that pupils experience a coherent education while the school considers its longer-term position with the care that position deserves.

Principle VII

Respect for the Character of the School

Every school has an established character which temporary cover should seek to understand before it seeks to influence.

Schools differ in ways which are readily visible and in ways which are understood only through experience. Their language, routines, pace, pastoral structures and expectations of pupils form a culture which cannot be reduced to a short briefing. A temporary teacher should therefore enter with attentiveness rather than presumption.

Respect does not require imitation, nor does it prevent the exercise of professional judgement. It requires recognition that the school has reasons for the way it conducts its work. Those reasons may not be immediately apparent to a visitor. Questions should be asked courteously, and differences in practice should not be mistaken for deficiencies merely because they are unfamiliar.

In Japanese schools, this attentiveness may extend to matters of communication, hierarchy, shared spaces, arrival, staffroom conduct and the place of English education within the wider curriculum. Experience of one institution should not be treated as a complete account of another.

The school remains the principal authority

The temporary teacher contributes professional expertise, but the school determines its policies, routines and educational priorities. Where an instruction is unclear, clarification should be sought. Where a genuine concern arises, it should be raised through the proper channel and expressed with care.

Respect for institutional character allows temporary cover to feel less like an interruption and more like a considered extension of the school’s own provision.

Principle VIII

Clear Communication When Circumstances Change

Changes should be communicated early, plainly and to the people responsible for acting upon them.

Temporary assignments are particularly susceptible to change. An absent teacher may return sooner than anticipated. A single day may become a week. A timetable may be revised, a class moved or an additional responsibility introduced. Such changes are not necessarily signs of poor organisation; they are part of the practical life of schools.

Difficulty arises when changes remain implicit. A revised expectation should be stated, considered and agreed. Neither the school nor the teacher should assume that availability, duties or suitability remain unchanged simply because the original assignment has already begun.

Honest communication may occasionally produce an inconvenient answer. A teacher may be unable to extend. A school may need a different form of expertise as the assignment develops. It is better to recognise these matters promptly than to preserve a superficial appearance of certainty.

Communication should be proportionate

Not every minor alteration requires ceremony. The standard is that information reaches the person who needs it, in sufficient time for a sensible response. During urgent circumstances, a concise, accurate message is generally of greater value than a lengthy explanation delivered too late.

Schools should know whom to contact, teachers should know from whom instructions are to be taken, and each party should be candid about matters which may affect the quality or feasibility of the assignment.

Principle IX

Standards Which Cannot Be Compromised

Urgency may alter what is possible. It does not alter what is acceptable.

Schools sometimes require assistance under difficult conditions. Information may be incomplete, notice may be short and the ideal arrangement may not be available. Professional judgement allows for these realities. It does not permit essential standards to be treated as optional.

Safeguarding obligations remain. Honesty remains. Suitable conduct remains. A teacher should not be placed where there is a material concern about their ability to carry out the work safely or responsibly. An assignment should not be described as confirmed when an essential condition remains unresolved.

Englishire will not promise universal availability, an impossible response time or a particular teacher before the relevant circumstances have been considered. Nor should a school be encouraged to regard temporary cover as a substitute for a permanent staffing decision which plainly requires separate attention.

Restraint is part of professional service

A responsible answer may be conditional. It may require further information. On occasion, it may be no. Such answers should not be regarded as a failure of service when they arise from proper concern for the school, the pupils or the integrity of the assignment.

Confidence is not built by promising that difficulties do not exist. It is built by describing them accurately and responding to them with sound judgement.

Principle X

Looking Beyond the Assignment

Good temporary cover should help a school manage the present without losing sight of what follows.

An individual assignment may be short, but the circumstances which produced it often have a longer history. Repeated absence, an extended recruitment process, timetable expansion or a recurring need for specialist teaching may indicate that the school would benefit from a broader contingency plan.

Temporary cover should create breathing room for such matters to be considered properly. It should not encourage a succession of hurried arrangements where a more deliberate solution is required. The value of cover lies partly in preserving stability whilst leadership decides what should happen next.

Where a relationship continues, each assignment should improve understanding. The school’s routines become clearer, briefing becomes more efficient and future decisions can be made with better information. Feedback should therefore be welcomed, recorded where appropriate and used to refine subsequent work.

A professional relationship is more useful than a transaction

Schools need not wait for an emergency before introducing themselves. A prior conversation about the timetable, location, age groups and likely requirements can make a later enquiry considerably more orderly. It also allows expectations to be discussed when neither party is under unnecessary pressure.

The most useful form of temporary cover is not one which simply arrives at short notice. It is one which helps the school retain its composure, preserve its standards and make the next decision with greater confidence than before.

Closing Declaration

The standard, stated plainly.

Every assignment deserves proper consideration. Every school deserves honest communication. Every classroom deserves a teacher who arrives prepared to uphold the trust placed in them.

This is the standard Englishire seeks to apply: continuity before convenience, suitability before availability, preparation before placement, and professional judgement throughout.
