The Hire You're Actually Making

By James Simmons · Published by Englishire Teacher Recruitment

A school posting a vacancy thinks it is filling a role. What it is actually doing, whether it realises it or not, is placing a long-term bet on continuity — and that bet is decided long before the first lesson is taught.

It is a strange thing about hiring in this industry: the decision that most determines a school's stability over the next two years is made in a single afternoon, often under time pressure, using an interview format that was never designed to answer the question that actually matters. Schools know this, in the abstract. Almost none of them act on it, because acting on it requires slowing down at precisely the moment everything in the business is telling them to speed up.

The System View

A CV answers one question: can this person teach English competently. For most candidates who make it to interview stage, the honest answer is yes — pedagogical competence is, relatively speaking, the easy part to screen for. A trial lesson, a few pointed questions about classroom management, a look at prior experience, and a school can arrive at a reasonably confident answer within an hour.

But that CV, and that interview, answer almost nothing about the question that actually determines outcomes over a two-year horizon: will this person still be in the classroom, functioning well, when the unglamorous parts of the job — visa renewals, cultural adjustment, a difficult term, a homesick winter — arrive. These are not edge cases. They are the default conditions of the job for any foreign teacher living and working in Japan for the first time, and they arrive on a schedule no interview can simulate. The paperwork frustrations of a visa renewal show up reliably in month eight or nine. The cultural disorientation tends to peak somewhere around the three-month mark, once the initial novelty has worn off and the daily friction of an unfamiliar workplace has had time to accumulate. None of this is exotic or unpredictable at the level of the industry. It is only unpredictable at the level of the individual candidate, which is exactly the level at which most hiring decisions are made.

Schools that hire on the first question and discover the second one too late are the ones cycling through turnover every few terms — not because they are hiring badly in any obvious sense, but because they are optimising for the wrong variable. The hire is not a staffing transaction. It is a forecast, and most schools are forecasting on incomplete information, using a data set — a CV, a thirty-minute interview, a demo lesson — that was built to answer a different question entirely.

The View From the Interview Room

Sit through enough hiring rounds at an eikaiwa and a pattern emerges that rarely shows up in the paperwork. The candidates who struggle are not usually the ones with weak English or thin teaching credentials — those are easy to screen out, and most schools do so effectively. They are the ones who interview beautifully: articulate, warm, clearly capable in front of a classroom, exactly the kind of candidate a hiring manager leaves the room feeling good about. And then, three months in, they discover that they underestimated what it means to be the only foreign staff member in a small Japanese workplace, navigating unfamiliar hierarchy, unfamiliar scheduling norms, and a classroom culture that doesn't match what their training prepared them for.

This is a specific and under-discussed kind of mismatch, distinct from a simple skills gap. A teacher who struggles with lesson planning can usually be coached; the gap is visible, nameable, and correctable with support. A teacher who is quietly, steadily worn down by disorientation — by the accumulated weight of not quite understanding office norms, not quite following a staff meeting, not quite knowing whom to ask for help — often doesn't present as a performance problem at all, until the moment they hand in their notice. From the school's perspective, the resignation can feel sudden. From the teacher's perspective, it rarely is. It is usually the visible end of a slow, largely invisible erosion that started somewhere around week two.

What makes this especially difficult for schools to screen for is that adaptability and charisma are not the same trait, and interviews are far better at surfacing the second than the first. A confident, articulate candidate can seem like an obvious hire precisely because those same qualities make for a good demo lesson — and yet confidence in a familiar setting predicts almost nothing about resilience in an unfamiliar one. The reverse is also true: a candidate who interviews a little awkwardly, whose English is polished but whose classroom presence is still developing, may turn out to be the one who handles a bureaucratic visa delay or a confusing staff hierarchy with unusual steadiness, simply because they've built that muscle before, somewhere else, under different circumstances.

The schools that hire well are often the ones asking less about pedagogy and more about how a candidate has handled disorientation before — a prior move abroad, a prior job in an unfamiliar culture, a period of genuine difficulty they had to work through without a clear playbook — because that, more than any single teaching skill, predicts whether they'll still be there next year. Pedagogy, in most cases, can be taught on the job. Adaptability is far harder to install after the fact.

The Operational Discipline

Reframed as a continuity decision rather than a recruitment task, hiring foreign teachers benefits from the same rigour applied to any high-consequence, hard-to-reverse choice — the kind of decision a well-run organisation in almost any field treats with structured process rather than gut instinct alone.

Screen for adaptability as a distinct criterion, not a soft afterthought to qualifications. Classroom management, cultural flexibility, and comfort with ambiguity predict retention more reliably than credentials alone, and yet most interview structures treat them as an informal impression gathered in passing rather than a criterion evaluated deliberately. This does not require elaborate psychometric testing. It can be as straightforward as asking specific, behavioural questions — not "are you adaptable?", which invites a rehearsed answer, but "tell me about a time you had to function in a system you didn't fully understand, and how you worked out what to do." The difference between a soft afterthought and a distinct criterion is often just the difference between an impression and a question asked on purpose.

Separate the hiring clock from the operational clock. Rushed hiring decisions cluster around staffing emergencies — exactly the conditions under which schools are least equipped to evaluate long-term fit, because the person conducting the interview is thinking less about who will thrive over two years and more about who can start teaching on Tuesday. This is a completely understandable pressure and a genuinely dangerous one, because it systematically biases hiring towards candidates who are simply available rather than candidates who are well suited. Building in interim cover — a temporary teacher, a redistributed timetable, a bridge arrangement of some kind — removes that time pressure from the decision itself, and allows a school to evaluate a candidate on the criteria that actually matter rather than the criterion of who happened to apply during a gap.

Plan for the hire's disruption, not just its success. Even a strong candidate, correctly screened and thoughtfully chosen, can be affected by a visa delay, a family emergency, or a change of circumstance no interview could have predicted. This is worth stating plainly because it cuts against a natural tendency to treat a careful hiring process as a guarantee. It isn't, and treating it as one sets a school up to be blindsided by an outcome that was, statistically, always somewhat likely. Treating disruption as a foreseeable risk, not a rare exception, is what separates schools that recover smoothly from those that scramble each time it happens. The good hiring process and the good contingency plan are not substitutes for each other. They are two separate disciplines, and a school needs both.

The Business Case

The economics here favour patience in a way that's easy to underweight when a classroom is short-staffed today and a warm body in front of the room feels, in the moment, like the only thing that matters. But the arithmetic of a bad hire is worse than it first appears, and worth walking through in full.

A rushed hire that doesn't work out costs a school the original recruitment spend — advertising, interviewing time, onboarding — and then, when the teacher leaves or is let go, a second recruitment cycle that duplicates nearly all of that cost. It also costs something harder to put a number on but arguably more expensive: a second disruption to the same pupils and parents who just absorbed the first one, at exactly the moment the school most needs to demonstrate that it has things under control. Two disruptions inside a single school year do more reputational damage than either would alone, because the second one confirms a suspicion the first one merely raised.

Schools that can afford to wait for the right candidate, because they have interim cover in place, are effectively buying insurance against the far larger cost of hiring twice. This is, in essence, the same logic that governs any decision where the cost of moving too fast is asymmetric to the cost of moving carefully: a slightly longer search is a small, visible, immediate cost. A bad hire is a larger, less visible, delayed cost that shows up months later, disguised as a resignation or a difficult performance conversation, and by then it's too late to have made a different choice.

It is this arithmetic that has made temporary bridge support increasingly common among schools navigating hiring decisions — not as a stopgap of last resort, brought in only after a school has already been caught short, but as the mechanism that makes a careful search possible in the first place. Framed this way, interim cover stops looking like an admission of a staffing failure and starts looking like what it actually is: a deliberate tool that buys a school the time to hire well, rather than a symptom of having hired poorly.

The Human Stakes

Behind every hiring decision is a classroom of pupils who will spend a year, or several, with whoever is chosen. This is easy to lose sight of in the middle of a recruitment process that, from the school's side, can start to feel like a purely administrative task — postings, CVs, interview slots, reference checks. But from a child's side, the hire is not administrative at all. It is the person who will greet them every week, correct their pronunciation, remember whether they had a piano recital last weekend, and become, over time, one of the fixed points of their childhood routine.

A good hire becomes a fixture of a child's routine — the person whose voice they associate with English, whose approval they seek, whose absence they would notice immediately, the way a child notices any small but meaningful absence in their week. A poor hire, or a hire made in haste to fill an emergency, risks becoming another disruption in a string of disruptions the pupils had no part in creating and have no ability to control. Children adapt, generally, to a new teacher. What they adapt to less easily is the pattern of teachers arriving and leaving in quick succession, each departure quietly teaching them not to get too attached to whoever is standing at the front of the room.

Getting the hire right is not, in the end, a CV exercise. It is a promise to a group of children about who will be showing up for them — and it deserves the time it takes to keep that promise well, even when every operational pressure in the moment is pushing a school towards the fastest available answer instead of the right one.

Reporting drawn from patterns observed across English-language schools in Japan navigating staffing continuity.
