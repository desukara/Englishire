How It Works

A clear process from first enquiry to final handover.

Schools should know what happens before cover begins.

Englishire approaches temporary teacher cover through a clear sequence of enquiry, review, confirmation, preparation, teaching and follow-up. Each stage exists to remove uncertainty before it reaches the classroom.

Before We Begin

Every assignment begins with a clear account of what the school requires.

A school making contact is describing more than a date and a gap in the timetable. It is explaining a temporary difficulty within an established educational environment.

The age of the learners, the nature of the lessons, the journey to the school, the materials available, the duration of the assignment and the expectations placed upon the teacher all influence whether the arrangement can be undertaken properly.

Our first task is to establish the facts: what is needed, what is known and what remains uncertain. We ask for the practical information required to assess the assignment clearly and identify any questions that must be resolved.

The process is deliberately straightforward. It is not intended to burden a school with administration. It exists so that the school, the teacher and Englishire share the same understanding before the first lesson begins.

A clear beginning is the first protection against a confused arrival.

From Enquiry to Handover

What happens after a school makes contact.

The sequence is practical, but the judgement within it matters.

  1. 01

    Listening

    The School Explains the Requirement

    The process begins with the circumstances as the school understands them. Cover may be required because of illness, leave, a recruitment gap, additional capacity or a programme of limited duration. The reason matters because it often determines the likely length, responsibilities and degree of continuity involved.

    We ask for the school location, requested dates, timetable, learner ages, approximate levels, class sizes, lesson format, available materials and any particular expectations. Where information is not yet known, the uncertainty should simply be stated.

    There is no need for ceremony. A concise and candid account is of far greater value than a polished description which leaves the essential questions unanswered.

    The school need not have solved the staffing difficulty before contacting us. It need only describe it as clearly as circumstances allow.

  2. 02

    Reviewing

    Englishire Reviews the Assignment as a Whole

    Once the details have been received, Englishire reviews the assignment as a whole. A timetable is not assessed in isolation from travel. A lesson format is not separated from the learners. A proposed duration is not separated from the continuity the school expects.

    We consider reporting time, stations and transfers, movement between classrooms, preparation periods, teaching materials, school routines, communication responsibilities and what the teacher may reasonably be expected to accomplish within the available time.

    This stage often reveals the practical questions that require clarification. It is better for those questions to arise now than on the morning of the assignment.

    An apparently minor detail may determine whether an arrangement is dependable in practice.

  3. 03

    Considering Suitability

    The Right Teacher Is Considered, Not Merely a Free One

    Availability is necessary, but it is not the standard by which a teacher should be selected. The relevant question is whether an available teacher is also appropriate for the learners, lesson format, school environment and responsibilities of the assignment.

    A teacher suited to adult conversation may not be the natural choice for a lively class of very young children. A confident generalist may not be appropriate for a specialist examination programme. A teacher whose journey is technically possible may still be unable to reach the school with sufficient reliability.

    Suitability therefore involves professional judgement rather than a simple diary search.

    A vacant timetable is not, by itself, a qualification.

  4. 04

    Confirming

    The Arrangement Is Set Out Clearly

    Where a suitable teacher is available and the assignment can be undertaken properly, the arrangement is confirmed. The dates, reporting time, location, lesson schedule, principal responsibilities and agreed practical terms should be clear to everyone concerned.

    An enquiry, discussion or expression of availability is not a confirmation. The school should know precisely when the arrangement has become definite, and any material change afterwards should be discussed rather than assumed.

    This protects the school from uncertainty and the teacher from arriving to an assignment different from the one accepted.

    Clarity at confirmation prevents avoidable negotiation at the school gate.

  5. 05

    Preparing

    The Teacher Receives a Useful Briefing

    Confirmation establishes the assignment. Preparation equips the teacher to carry it out. Before arrival, the teacher should receive the timetable, reporting instructions, school contact, lesson information, learner details, materials, classroom expectations and any procedures that bear upon the work.

    Not every school requires a lengthy briefing document. What matters is that the essential information reaches the teacher in a form that can be understood and used.

    Where cover extends beyond a single day, the briefing may also need to address homework, learner progress, reporting, communication with colleagues and continuity between lessons.

    Professional teacher cover begins before the classroom door opens.

  6. 06

    Supporting

    The Assignment Is Conducted With Calm Communication

    On the agreed day, the teacher reports according to the confirmed arrangements, allows appropriate time for introductions and prepares for the first lesson. The aim is not to announce the temporary nature of the arrangement, but to help the school day proceed with as little disturbance as possible.

    During longer assignments, circumstances may change. Materials may be revised, classes may move, an absence may be extended or the school may identify further responsibilities. Such changes should be communicated promptly and considered properly.

    Flexibility is valuable. Vagueness is not. The distinction is maintained through timely, proportionate communication.

    The best temporary cover is often the cover that permits the school to concentrate upon everything else.

  7. 07

    Handing Over

    The Assignment Concludes Without Losing What Was Learned

    A single-day assignment may require little more than a clear note concerning work completed and any matter the returning teacher should know. A longer placement may call for a more substantial handover of learner progress, materials, homework, changes to the timetable and outstanding responsibilities.

    Where further cover is required, the next stage is discussed rather than presumed. Dates, responsibilities and availability may have changed, and each continuation deserves the same clarity as the original arrangement.

    A proper conclusion protects continuity beyond the temporary teacher's final lesson.

    Temporary work should end cleanly, not simply cease.

What Helps at the Outset

Useful information allows a useful answer.

Urgent circumstances do not always permit every detail to be assembled before contact is made. Schools are welcome to send what is known and identify what remains uncertain.

Nevertheless, the following information allows the assignment to be considered with fewer assumptions and often with greater efficiency.

School and contact details
The school name, address, nearest station and the person responsible for the enquiry.
Dates and timetable
Requested dates, reporting time, lesson periods, breaks, finish time and movement between locations.
Learners and classes
Ages, approximate levels, class sizes and any needs or circumstances relevant to the teacher.
Lessons and materials
Objectives, teaching format, materials, work already completed, homework and reporting expectations.
School procedures
Arrival, dress, conduct, safeguarding, classroom routines and communication arrangements.
The likely duration
Whether the requirement is isolated, recurring, open-ended or expected to conclude on a particular date.

When the Answer May Be No

Occasionally, declining is the more responsible decision.

Not every enquiry can or should become an assignment. A school is better served by a prompt and honest answer than by a convenient promise followed by uncertainty.

A decision to decline does not suggest that the requirement lacks importance. It means only that we are not satisfied the work can be undertaken to an appropriate standard under the circumstances presented.

  • The essential requirements remain unclear The assignment cannot be judged properly without sufficient information concerning the timetable, learners or responsibilities.
  • No suitably matched teacher is available A free teacher is not necessarily an appropriate teacher.
  • The journey makes punctual attendance uncertain An arrangement should remain dependable in ordinary Tokyo travel conditions.
  • The expectations fall outside appropriate expertise Specialist work deserves a teacher with the relevant capability rather than an improvised substitute.
  • The terms change the nature of the assignment A material change may require the arrangement to be reconsidered rather than treated as automatically accepted.

What the Process Protects

Clarity for the school, preparedness for the teacher and continuity for the learner.

The School

The school should understand what has been agreed, who is arriving, what information remains required and how changes will be handled.

The Teacher

The teacher should receive sufficient information to arrive punctually, understand the environment and carry out the work with professional confidence.

The Learner

The learner should experience a purposeful lesson and the greatest degree of continuity that temporary circumstances reasonably permit.

Every stage returns to the same standard: suitability before convenience, preparation before placement and communication before assumption.

Practical Questions

Matters schools quite reasonably ask.

These answers concern the process itself. The fuller questions page addresses the wider service in greater detail.

How quickly will the school receive an answer?

This depends upon the urgency, clarity and complexity of the enquiry. We respond as promptly as circumstances permit, but the assignment is not confirmed until Englishire has expressly confirmed it.

May we make contact before cover is required?

Certainly. An advance introduction can be useful, particularly where a school has recurring requirements or a timetable that would benefit from prior understanding.

What happens if some details are not yet known?

Send what is available and state what remains uncertain. We can then explain which details are essential before the arrangement can be reviewed or confirmed.

Does making an enquiry guarantee cover?

No. Cover depends upon the requirements, teacher suitability and availability. We do not promise an arrangement before we are satisfied it can be undertaken properly.

Can the arrangement change after confirmation?

Changes may be possible, though they should be discussed. A revised timetable, location, learner group or duration may affect suitability and availability.

What should the teacher receive before arrival?

At minimum, the confirmed timetable, location, reporting instructions, contact details, lesson information and any important school procedures or expectations.
